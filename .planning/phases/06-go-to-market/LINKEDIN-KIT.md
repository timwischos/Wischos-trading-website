### Week 1 — Establish Expertise

**Post 1 — Tuesday, Week 1**
Format: Text
Pillar: Material Education

> Most corporate gift buyers don't know they're choosing between two completely different metals when they pick "silver" gifts.
>
> Stainless steel and zinc alloy both look silver. Both photograph similarly in catalogue images. The cost difference is roughly 40–60% per unit.
>
> What's different:
>
> Stainless steel — heavier in hand, resistant to rust and tarnishing over years of use, holds engraving cleanly, standard in premium B2B gifts.
>
> Zinc alloy — lighter, lower cost, used for cast shapes that are hard to achieve in steel, more prone to surface wear over time, common in promotional merchandise.
>
> Neither material is wrong — they serve different purposes. But a buyer who doesn't know the difference will order from a catalogue image and be surprised by what arrives.
>
> When you're requesting a sample or reviewing a product spec, ask: what is the base material? What surface treatment is applied? How is the finish maintained over time?
>
> Those three questions separate a considered purchase from a catalogue order.

**Post 6 — Friday, Week 2**
Format: Text
Pillar: Process/Transparency

> What actually happens between "yes I want samples" and opening the sample box.
>
> Most buyers expect: supplier sends sample, sample arrives.
>
> What actually happens:
>
> **Step 1 — Brief confirmation.** Before a sample is made, the supplier needs to confirm material, finish, engraving method, and packaging type. Vague briefs at this stage lead to samples that miss the intent.
>
> **Step 2 — Sample production.** For metal gifts, sample production takes 5–7 working days. This is a handmade unit, not pulled from inventory. If you need a specific colour or finish, confirm the RAL or Pantone reference before this step starts.
>
> **Step 3 — Sample shipment.** Samples are typically shipped by express courier (DHL, FedEx, or similar). Transit time from Guangdong to Australia: 3–5 working days. To the EU: 5–7 working days.
>
> **Step 4 — Approval.** When the sample arrives, check: weight and feel in hand, surface finish quality, engraving depth and placement, packaging fit and construction. Approve by return email with specific written confirmation. Do not give verbal or informal approval — it creates ambiguity later.
>
> **Step 5 — Production deposit.** Typically 30–50% to start production. The clock for lead time starts here, not when you first contacted the supplier.
>
> The sample stage is where most order delays happen — not in production. A clear brief and a fast approval cycle compress the total timeline significantly.

## Section 5: Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Personal profile written for resume readers, not buyers.** If your headline says "Founder at Wischos Gift" with nothing else, your ICP buyer reads it and moves on. Fix: your headline should answer "what problem do you solve for me today?" before they click your profile.

2. **Company page created before personal profile is optimized.** Original posts live on your personal profile. The company page reposts and provides SEO anchor. If your personal profile is weak, every post underperforms regardless of company page quality. Fix: personal profile first, every time.

3. **Posting company announcements instead of buyer-useful content.** "We are excited to launch our new product line" posts generate zero engagement from your ICP. Fix: 85% of posts should educate, help, or share genuine expertise. Maximum 15% promotional content. The sales result comes from being seen as a credible source before the buyer has a purchasing need — not from announcing products.

4. **Inconsistent brand name across LinkedIn fields.** Use "Wischos Gift" in every field: company name, mentions in posts, About section, Experience entry. Never "Wischos International", "Wischos Trading", or "WG". Inconsistency fragments any brand recognition being built and creates confusion for buyers who search for you after seeing a post.
